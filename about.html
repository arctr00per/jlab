ORIGINS

In 2017 I walked into Virgil Abloh's EM PTY GALLERY on Mercer Street, a retail space dressed as a forest, fall foliage and trees and orange industrial racks holding clothes alongside outdoor building supplies. Nothing was spared. It wasn't just a store, it was world-building. That visit cracked something open for me: how much more there is to an object than the object itself, and how sound, space, display, and detail can come together to create something that stays with you. Virgil also proved that a designer need not be confined to one medium. He moved between sculpture, architecture, graphic design, and music, carrying a distinct voice throug it all. That's the designer I set out to be.

ART

School gave me the space to follow every curiosity. I studied and made things across architecture, drawing, robotics, programming, photography, and videography, not to collect skills, but because ideas rarely fit neatly into one medium. What developed alongside that breadth was a deep commitment to craft and storytelling: the belief that details aren't finishing touches, they're where meaning lives. This photo sculpture from my senior year at UCLA is one of my favorite examples of that, an idea that needed to be built, not just designed.

COMMERCE

My professional work has taken me inside some of the most exciting young consumer brands being built today. Through advising and consulting, I've developed a clear-eyed sense of what it actually takes to scale an idea, and what gets lost when you do it without care. The entrepreneurs I've worked alongside are among the most passionate people I've met, building products with real innovation and real things to say. Working with them has only deepened my belief that the story behind a brand matters as much as anything it makes.

BRIDGING THE GAP

Through design-led brands, there is an opportunity to bridge the gap between art and commerce. Three names that serve as guideposts of how to inhabit it well are Teenage Engineering, Dana Lee Brown, and Upstate Diary. Teenage Engineering makes music devices of uncommon beauty, tools for limitless creative output that feel as considered as any sculpture. Dana Lee Brown tracks every material input soil-to-loom, building timeless pieces with a complete story behind them. Upstate Diary shines a light on people living and making thoughtfully, a reminder that the philosophy behind the work matters as much as the output. What connects them is care. I've always believed that while people may not notice all the details, they can always feel perfection, and when something is deeply considered down to the last detail, it creates a lasting experience.

SOME MORE

Art is one of my deepest influences, and Ed Ruscha's Year After Year (1973) is a work I always come back to: just the words, white sans-serif, slightly above center, on blue pastel. Ruscha was commercially trained and it shows: his paintings carry the immediacy of advertising while asking something deeper. Those three words, in that context, become a philosophical question about time. That balance, immediate and lingering, is what I'm always chasing.
